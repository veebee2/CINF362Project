Invasive Species Management

Restoring balance to New York's ecosystems by identifying and removing non-native threats.

Invasive plants now occupy millions of acres across New York, costing tens of millions of dollars each year in management, lost agricultural productivity, and damaged infrastructure. Understanding which species to watch for and how to remove them properly is one of the most important things a New Yorker can do for the local environment.

Best Invasive Removal Practices

Manual Extraction

For species like Garlic Mustard, hand-pulling before seeds drop is the most effective way to clear small areas without chemicals. The key is to pull when the soil is moist so the entire root comes up, and to bag and dispose of pulled plants. Never compost them.

Solarization

Covering cleared soil with black plastic tarps during the summer months uses heat to kill off remaining invasive root fragments and seeds. This method works best for sunny, level sites and typically requires the tarp to stay in place for an entire growing season.

Cut-Stump Treatment

For woody invasives like Tree of Heaven and Asian Bittersweet, cutting the stem and immediately applying a targeted herbicide to the freshly cut surface is far more effective than spraying foliage. This approach uses a small amount of chemical applied precisely where it is needed.

Biological Control

For widespread invasives like Purple Loosestrife and Japanese Knotweed, scientists have introduced specialized insects from the plants' native ranges that feed only on those species. New York approved the release of the knotweed psyllid in 2020 as the first biocontrol agent for Japanese Knotweed in the country.

Persistence and Replanting

Whichever method you use, plan to revisit the site for at least 3 to 5 years. Even a single missed plant or root fragment can restart an infestation. After removal, replant the cleared area with native species to prevent reinvasion.

Native Pollinator Gardens

The final step in conservation is replacement. Once invasives are gone, planting NY-native flowers ensures that pollinators have a stable food source. A well-planned pollinator garden is more than just pretty. It functions as a mini-ecosystem, producing nectar and pollen from early spring through late fall.

  • Step 1: Clear the site of all non-native debris.
  • Step 2: Test your soil for pH and moisture levels.
  • Step 3: Select NY-native species that bloom in succession from April to October.
  • Step 4: Plant in clusters of three or more of the same species rather than scattering individuals. Pollinators conserve energy by visiting many flowers of the same type in one trip.
  • Step 5: Skip the mulch layer in some areas. About 70% of native bees nest in bare ground.
  • Step 6: Leave stems and seed heads standing through winter. Many native bees overwinter inside hollow plant stems.

Even a small garden, a single 4 by 8 foot bed, can host hundreds of pollinator visits per day during peak bloom. Scaled across a neighborhood, these gardens form crucial corridors that allow pollinators to move safely through developed landscapes.
