Protecting Local Biodiversity

Supporting local ecosystems through sustainable planting

What are Native Plants?

Native plants are species that have occurred naturally in a particular region, ecosystem, or habitat without human introduction. In New York, these plants have co-evolved over thousands of years with local wildlife.

This long shared history matters. Native bees, butterflies, birds, and mammals have adapted alongside these plants in ways that cannot be easily replaced. A monarch caterpillar, for example, can only digest milkweed leaves — no other plant in the world will keep it alive. When we lose a native plant, we don't just lose a pretty flower; we lose a piece of the food web that countless species rely on.

New York's native flora is incredibly diverse because of the state's varied geography. From the alpine meadows of the High Peaks to the coastal plains of Long Island, different regions of New York support different native plant communities. A plant native to the Adirondacks may not be native to Manhattan, even though they're in the same state.

Why and How to Preserve Native Plants

Preserving native flora is vital for maintaining healthy soil, clean water, and food for local wildlife. Native plants have deep, established root systems that hold soil in place during heavy rains, filter pollutants before they reach waterways, and store carbon underground to help fight climate change.

You can help by:

  • Removing invasive species from your yard before they go to seed.
  • Reducing lawn space in favor of native garden beds and meadow patches.
  • Avoiding pesticides that harm the insects relying on these plants.
  • Buying plants from nurseries that specifically grow New York native species.
  • Leaving fallen leaves and stems in garden beds over winter — many native bees and butterflies overwinter inside them.
  • Avoiding cultivars labeled "nativars" that have been bred for unusual colors but may no longer support pollinators properly.

Conservation does not require land ownership. Renters can grow native plants in containers on a balcony, students can volunteer with restoration groups, and anyone can advocate for native landscaping in parks, schools, and public spaces.

Common Native Plant Species

Below are some of the most recognizable and ecologically important native plants you can grow in New York. Each one supports specific pollinators and wildlife, and most are easy to establish in a home garden.

Common Milkweed

Asclepias syriaca

The single most important plant for monarch butterflies — their caterpillars can eat nothing else. Common milkweed grows 3 to 5 feet tall, produces fragrant pink-purple flower clusters from June through August, and supports over 450 species of insects in total. It thrives in full sun and tolerates poor, dry soils, making it ideal for sunny corners of any yard.

Black-Eyed Susan

Rudbeckia hirta

A hardy, drought-resistant flower that blooms from June through October, providing one of the longest nectar seasons of any native wildflower. Its bright yellow petals and dark central cone attract bees, butterflies, and beneficial wasps. After flowering, the seed heads feed goldfinches, chickadees, and other small songbirds well into winter.

Goldenrod

Solidago canadensis

Often unfairly blamed for hay fever (the real culprit is ragweed), goldenrod is one of the most valuable late-season nectar sources in New York. Its bright yellow plumes feed migrating monarchs, native bees stocking up before winter, and dozens of specialist pollinator species. Goldenrod supports more insect species than nearly any other perennial wildflower.

New England Aster

Symphyotrichum novae-angliae

Tall, vivid purple blooms appear from late August through October, providing crucial fuel for monarch butterflies during their long migration to Mexico. New England aster also hosts over 100 species of native bees and is a host plant for the pearl crescent butterfly. It pairs beautifully with goldenrod in late-season pollinator gardens.

Wild Bergamot (Bee Balm)

Monarda fistulosa

A member of the mint family with shaggy lavender flowers that bloom in midsummer. Wild bergamot is a magnet for hummingbirds, bumblebees, and hummingbird moths thanks to its tubular flowers. The leaves give off a strong oregano-like scent when crushed and have been used in traditional teas. Resistant to deer browsing.

Cardinal Flower

Lobelia cardinalis

One of New York's most striking native plants, cardinal flower produces brilliant red spikes that bloom from July through September. Its tubular shape is specifically adapted for ruby-throated hummingbirds, which are nearly the only pollinators able to reach its nectar. Best grown in moist soils near streams, ponds, or rain gardens.

Native Pollinators

New York is home to a remarkable diversity of pollinators, from tiny native sweat bees to migratory hummingbirds. These creatures rely on native plants for survival, and native plants in turn rely on them for reproduction. The relationship is so tight that the loss of either side puts the other at risk.

Ruby-Throated Hummingbird

The only hummingbird species that breeds in the eastern United States, ruby-throats arrive in New York each spring after a remarkable nonstop flight across the Gulf of Mexico. They visit hundreds of flowers a day, favoring red and orange tubular blooms like cardinal flower, wild bergamot, and trumpet honeysuckle. A single hummingbird can consume up to twice its body weight in nectar daily.

Karner Blue Butterfly

This tiny endangered butterfly is named after Karner, New York, a hamlet just outside Albany where it was first scientifically described in the 1860s. With a wingspan of just one inch, the Karner blue depends entirely on a single native plant — wild blue lupine — as the sole food source for its caterpillars. Habitat loss and the suppression of natural fires have caused populations to collapse by over 90% from their historical numbers. The butterfly was placed on New York's endangered species list in 1977 and the federal endangered species list in 1992. The Albany Pine Bush Preserve is one of the last places on Earth where this species can still be reliably seen, making it a uniquely New York conservation story.

Native Bees

New York is home to more than 450 species of wild bees, the vast majority of which are native and non-honeybees. Most are solitary, do not produce honey, and do not sting. They include bumblebees, mason bees, leafcutter bees, mining bees, and tiny sweat bees. Native bees are far more efficient pollinators than honeybees for many crops — a single mason bee can do the work of about 100 honeybees in an apple orchard. Many native bees are specialists that can only feed on a single genus of native plant, meaning the loss of those plants directly threatens their survival.

Monarch Butterfly

Perhaps the most recognizable insect in North America, the monarch undertakes one of the longest insect migrations on the planet — up to 3,000 miles from New York to central Mexico each fall. Their entire lifecycle depends on milkweed: females will only lay eggs on milkweed leaves, and the caterpillars cannot survive on any other plant. Monarch populations have declined by more than 80% since the 1990s, largely due to the loss of milkweed across their range. Planting native milkweed is the single most impactful action a New York gardener can take to support monarchs.
