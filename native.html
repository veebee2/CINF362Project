Protecting Local Biodiversity

Supporting local ecosystems through sustainable planting

What are Native Plants?

Native plants are species that have occurred naturally in a particular region, ecosystem, or habitat without human introduction. In New York, these plants have co-evolved over thousands of years with local wildlife.

This long shared history matters. Native bees, butterflies, birds, and mammals have adapted alongside these plants in ways that cannot be easily replaced. A monarch caterpillar, for example, can only digest milkweed leaves. No other plant in the world will keep it alive. When we lose a native plant, we don't just lose a pretty flower, we lose a piece of the food web that countless species rely on.

New York's native flora is incredibly diverse because of the state's varied geography. From the alpine meadows of the High Peaks to the coastal plains of Long Island, different regions of New York support different native plant communities.

Why and How to Preserve Native Plants

Preserving native flora is vital for maintaining healthy soil, clean water, and food for local wildlife. Native plants have deep, established root systems that hold soil in place during heavy rains, filter pollutants before they reach waterways, and store carbon underground to help fight climate change.

You can help by:

  • Removing invasive species from your yard.
  • Reducing lawn space in favor of native garden beds.
  • Avoiding pesticides that harm the insects relying on these plants.
  • Buying plants from nurseries that grow New York native species.
  • Leaving fallen leaves and stems in garden beds over winter, since many native bees and butterflies overwinter inside them.

Conservation does not require land ownership. Renters can grow native plants in containers on a balcony, students can volunteer with restoration groups, and anyone can advocate for native landscaping in parks and public spaces.

Common Native Plant Species

Milkweed

The single most important plant for monarch butterflies, since their caterpillars can eat nothing else. Milkweed grows 3 to 5 feet tall, produces fragrant pink-purple flower clusters from June through August, and supports over 450 species of insects in total.

Black-Eyed Susan

A hardy flower that attracts bees and butterflies. It blooms from June through October, providing one of the longest nectar seasons of any native wildflower. After flowering, the seed heads feed goldfinches and other small songbirds well into winter.

Goldenrod

Provides late-season nectar for pollinators. Often unfairly blamed for hay fever, the real culprit is ragweed. Goldenrod is one of the most valuable late-season nectar sources in New York and supports more insect species than nearly any other perennial wildflower.

New England Aster

Tall purple blooms appear from late August through October, providing crucial fuel for monarch butterflies during their long migration to Mexico. New England aster also hosts over 100 species of native bees and pairs beautifully with goldenrod in pollinator gardens.

Wild Bergamot

A member of the mint family with shaggy lavender flowers that bloom in midsummer. Wild bergamot is a magnet for hummingbirds, bumblebees, and hummingbird moths thanks to its tubular flowers. The leaves give off a strong oregano-like scent when crushed.

Cardinal Flower

One of New York's most striking native plants. Cardinal flower produces brilliant red spikes that bloom from July through September. Its tubular shape is specifically adapted for ruby-throated hummingbirds, which are nearly the only pollinators able to reach its nectar.

Native Pollinators

New York is home to diverse pollinators, including the Ruby-throated Hummingbird, the Karner Blue Butterfly, and over 450 species of wild bees. These creatures rely on native plants for survival.

The Karner Blue Butterfly is named after Karner, New York, a hamlet just outside Albany where it was first scientifically described in the 1860s. With a wingspan of just one inch, the Karner blue depends entirely on a single native plant, wild blue lupine, as the sole food source for its caterpillars. Habitat loss has caused populations to collapse by over 90% from their historical numbers, and the butterfly was placed on the federal endangered species list in 1992. The Albany Pine Bush Preserve is one of the last places on Earth where this species can still be reliably seen.

The monarch butterfly undertakes one of the longest insect migrations on the planet, up to 3,000 miles from New York to central Mexico each fall. Their entire lifecycle depends on milkweed. Females will only lay eggs on milkweed leaves, and the caterpillars cannot survive on any other plant. Monarch populations have declined by more than 80% since the 1990s, largely due to the loss of milkweed across their range.

Most of New York's 450+ native bee species are solitary, do not produce honey, and do not sting. They include bumblebees, mason bees, leafcutter bees, and tiny sweat bees. Many native bees are specialists that can only feed on a single genus of native plant, meaning the loss of those plants directly threatens their survival.
