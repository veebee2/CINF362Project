Best Practices for Native Plant Conservation

Taking care of your local ecosystem does not require a lot of space or experience. Whether you have a backyard, a balcony, or just a window box, there are simple steps you can take to support native plants and the wildlife that depends on them.

Planting Native Species

One of the most impactful things you can do is swap out non-native ornamental plants for native alternatives. Native plants are already adapted to New York's climate and soil, meaning they need less watering, fewer fertilizers, and far less upkeep once established.

  • Research which plants are native to your specific region of New York State.
  • Purchase from local nurseries that specialize in native species.
  • Start small — even one or two native plants make a difference for local pollinators.
  • Group plants together to create patches of habitat rather than isolated specimens.

Removing Invasive Species

Invasive plants outcompete native species for sunlight, water, and nutrients. Removing them from your yard gives native plants the room they need to thrive. Always dispose of invasive plants properly so they cannot spread to new areas.

  • Pull invasive plants by the root before they go to seed whenever possible.
  • Bag and dispose of invasive material — do not compost it.
  • Check with your local municipality for invasive species drop-off programs.
  • Be consistent — many invasive species require multiple seasons of removal to fully control.

Reducing Pesticide and Herbicide Use

Many common pesticides and herbicides harm the very insects that native plants depend on for pollination. Reducing or eliminating their use in your yard protects the full chain of life that native plants support.

  • Use targeted, organic alternatives when pest control is truly necessary.
  • Avoid broad-spectrum insecticides that kill beneficial insects alongside pests.
  • Let natural predators like birds and ladybugs handle pest populations where possible.

Rethinking Your Lawn

Traditional grass lawns offer very little ecological value. Converting even a portion of your lawn into a native plant garden bed can dramatically increase the biodiversity of your yard and reduce the environmental cost of lawn maintenance.

  • Replace sections of turf with native ground covers like wild ginger or creeping phlox.
  • Create a pollinator garden using native flowering plants in a sunny area of your yard.
  • Leave leaf litter in garden beds over winter — many native insects overwinter in it.

Spreading Awareness in Your Community

Individual action matters, but collective action matters more. Sharing what you know with neighbors, friends, and local organizations can multiply your impact far beyond your own yard.

  • Share native plant resources with neighbors and local community groups.
  • Participate in local restoration events hosted by conservation organizations.
  • Advocate for native plantings in public spaces like parks and school grounds.
  • Report invasive species sightings to the NYS Invasive Species Reporting system.
